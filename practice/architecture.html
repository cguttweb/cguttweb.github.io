History of Architecture

Many people think of it as a modern phenomenon but in reality humans have been 'building' since prehistoric times e.g. Skara Brae in the Orkney Islands which dates to the Neolithic period. It is visible all around us from the everyday terrace house to stately homes and castles.

Architecture is defined as the process of and the product of planning, designing and constructing buildings and other physical structures.

The earliest surviving written work on architecture is De architectura, by the Roman architect Vitruvius in the early 1st century AD.

Today it is regarded as a profession, but despite its prehistoric origins professional training only began c.200 years ago. Before this it was usually noble 'gentlemen' who took on the mantel of an architect usually as a hobby.

Branches of architecture are civil, military, sacred, naval and landscape.

The Classical Style

This well-known style started in Greece c.2600 years ago and was first used in Greek temples. Ionic 600BC in Turkey. Corinthian order was used by Romans main difference was the different capital or architrave. Although it began in Greece style was developed and elaborated on by Romans most visually through the use of the Corinthian Order.
There were 5 orders made into set to represent the most perfect form (Doric, Ionic, Corinithian, Tuscan, Composite) most notable in columns proportion, symmetry and relationship between individual parts to the whole also help characterise classicism. After the collapse of the western Roman empire architectural traditions no longer practised in most of western Europe. Romans were the first to use archs to fully exploit their use.

Classical revival began in 15th century 1420s in Italy which influenced western architecture for the next 400 years seen as representing strength and dignity favoured for use in buildings of cultural and political authority e.g. town halls. It 'loosely' describes architecture with classical elements examples can be seen throughout the period and into 19th and 20th centuries.

Byzantine architecture

The most famous example is the Hagia Sophia in Istanbul. It began as a mosque was then a church and is now a museum. It was built by emperor Constantine the Great.

Romanesque architecture

There is no clear defined date as to when this style first appeared dates range from 6th to 10th century. It is a style in medieval Europe characterised by semi-circular arches.

Romanesque according to the Oxford English Dictionary means "descended from Roman".

Gothic Style

This style originated in France in 1140s; it developed from the Romanesque style based on pointed arch and rib vaults e.g. Reims cathedral and continued until c.1500. The structure relied on ribbed vaults – ribs carried the weight held in place by a boss e.g. Westminster Abbey (1246-59).
Gothic was first used as a derogatory term as the 17th century Goth meant 'vandal' in English a despoiler with German heritage therefore this was applied to the architectural styles of northern Europe. Common features are the rib vault, buttresses including flying buttresses, large windows grouped together or have tracery (?), rose windows, towers, spires and pinnacles and ornate facades. Examples include Rheinstein Castle (Germany) and the Cathedral of Saint-Gatian.

Gothic revival origins lie in England in 1730s nostalgia also revived the medieval techniques of stained glass e.g. Holy Trinity church and by 1840s style associated with moral vaules of Christian belief. Palace of Westminster one of largest Gothic revival buildings in the world.

Neoclassical

This is the term used to describe the academic revival of classicism which began in France in 1760s. The restudying of classical buildings became popular as the result of increased interest in archaeology and antiquarism – Pompeii and Herculaneum excavations and the rediscovery of ancient Greece. This style is characterised by the severity of appearance and solidity – orders used structurally.

Early Modern/International Style

Modernism

This developed in central Europe following the end of WWI. It rejected historical styles and instead focused on clean lines and simple forms. This also includes Futurism, Constructivism, De Stiji and Bauhaus.
Architects saw buildings as machines and designed them to be efficient e.g. flat roofs. It saw the use of industrially produced materials i.e. steel, sheet glass and reinforced concrete simplistic with colours and decoration kept to a minimum. Bauhaus Weimar 1919 became major influence on modernism internationally. Gropius was a key figure as the 1st director of highly influential Bauhaus movement.

High Tech

This style exploited the industrial and functional aspects of building design e.g. German Pavilion (1929 – International Exposition). Industrial look through the use of flat roofs, intersecting walls and grid of steel columns.

Postmodernism

1960+ it started as a reaction to the modern movement looks to historical styles for inspiration. It has the use of modern methods but with stylistic devices like classical columns and pediments. Key influence was the 1966 book Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture by American architect Robert Venturi.

Architects

The word architect comes from the latin architectus which derives from the Greek arkhi meaning chief and tekton meaning builder therefore an architect is a chief builder.

There are many, many architects who deserve a mention in this section. The first ever recorded architect was Imhotep from Ancient Egypt.

As one of the most acclaimed English architects of all time on this St Georges Day, it feels appropriate to start with Sir Christopher Wren.

He is famous the world over as the man who rebuilt St Pauls Cathedral in London following the Great Fire in 1666; this project spanned his entire architectural career from 1675 until its completion in 1711. He was also responsible for other notable buildings such as the Royal Naval College in Greenwich and the Wren building of the College of William and Mary in Williamsburg, Virginia; which is the oldest academic building in continuous use in the US. He was appointed the King's Surveyor of Works in 1669 and knighted in 1673. He was also a founder of the Royal Society and was its president between 1660 and 1682. In 1696 he was given the position of Surveyor of Greenwich Naval Hospital and in 1698 he was appointed Surveyor of Westminster Abbey.
